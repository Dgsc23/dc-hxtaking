Interview & Communication 19

Q. Know the 3 main functions of the medical interview (Cole & Bird).The medical interview has 3 main functions (Cole & Bird): (1) Build the relationship, (2) Assess and understand, (3) Collaborate for management. Build the relationship = develop rapport, establish mutual respect, establish trust, and convey empathy and compassion. Assess and understand = questioning and data gathering. Collaborate for management = patient education. During the interview and physical exam you gather data to generate a hypothesis for the cause of the problem and collect information to develop a management plan; this helps generate a differential diagnosis (road map to the cause) and the primary diagnosis. Osler: 'Listen to your patient, they might be telling you their diagnosis.' The SOAP/HPI deck states 80-90% of the diagnosis comes from the history (the Intro deck cites ~80%).
Q. Know the definitions of empathy, compassion, and sympathy, and the role of non-verbal communication.Empathy = the ability to understand and share the feelings of another; putting yourself in the other person's shoes. Compassion = 'sympathetic consciousness of others' distress together with a desire to alleviate it.' Sympathy = entering into or sharing another's feelings; feeling pity or sorrow for another's hardships. The nonverbal behavior of the physician contributes significantly to the overall quality of the doctor-patient relationship. Quiet, attentive listening conveys interest and builds rapport more powerfully than virtually any other action or utterance the physician can make. Doctors who establish appropriate eye contact are more likely to detect emotional distress; doctors who lean forward and have an open body posture have more satisfied patients. From the patient's perspective, listening is a defining feature of a 'good' doctor (enables accurate diagnoses, maintains the relationship, acts as a therapeutic agent); good relationships lead to patients who are more satisfied and more likely to adhere to treatment (Roter & Hall, 2009).
Q. Know the three therapeutic qualities (Carl Rogers).Borrowed from psychologist Carl Rogers, three therapeutic qualities observed in practitioners with good clinical outcomes are Respect, Genuineness, and Empathy. Respect means accepting your patient as they are, not judging, and separating your personal feelings; Genuineness means being yourself and acknowledging your position as a student or lack of knowledge (e.g., referring out).
Q. Know how empathy differs from sympathy.Empathy is the ability to understand and share the feelings of another - putting yourself in the other person's shoes - and is compared/contrasted with sympathy. Brene Brown: 'Rarely does a response make something better. What makes something better is a connection.'
Q. Know the four qualities of empathy.The four qualities of empathy are: Perspective Taking (validate the other's experience; their perspective is their truth), Staying out of Judgement, Recognizing the emotion in the other person, and Communicating the emotion back ('I hear you; I see you; I get it').
Q. Know the evidence that empathy improves patient outcomes.Patients who received emotional support/education after surgery or MI had fewer complications and were discharged about 2 days earlier than those who did not. More empathetic/sympathetic practitioners have more satisfied patients, and satisfied patients are more likely to comply; an 8-hour communication training program improved care without lengthening visits.
Q. Know Coulehan's levels of empathic response.Coulehan's levels of response: positive levels are Interchangeable (accurately recognize and assess feelings/symptoms the patient expresses) and Additive (recognize both what the patient expresses and what they feel but do not express). Negative levels are Ignoring (not responding to the cognitive or emotional content) and Minimizing (responding at a lesser level than the patient's expressed concern).
Q. Know the balance of efficiency and empathy in the interview.As many as three quarters of all diagnoses can be made by history alone. A delicate balance of Efficiency, Empathy, and Efficacy: gather enough information in a timely manner, validate the patient's experience, and arrive at a conclusion so the patient leaves satisfied.

Q. According to the slides, how is empathy defined?The ability to understand and share the feelings of another; putting yourself in their shoes — Empathy = ability to understand and share another's feelings; putting yourself in their shoes.
Q. Which definition matches 'compassion' per the slides?Sympathetic consciousness of others' distress together with a desire to alleviate it — Compassion is defined as 'sympathetic consciousness of others' distress together with a desire to alleviate it.'
Q. The three therapeutic qualities of the practitioner, borrowed from Carl Rogers, are:Respect, Genuineness, and Empathy — Carl Rogers' qualities: Respect, Genuineness, Empathy.

Cultural Competence & Humility 9

Q. Know the definitions of culture and intersectionality.Culture is the customary beliefs, social forms, and material traits of a racial, religious, or social group, plus shared attitudes, values, goals, and practices; it is affected by politics, class, gender, religion, age, and ethnicity. Intersectionality describes how race, class, gender, and other characteristics intersect and overlap, creating unique sets of experiences; understanding it helps you understand your patient's experience better.
Q. Know the difference between cultural competence and cultural humility.Cultural competence (Bates) is a set of attitudes, skills, behaviors, and policies that enable organizations and staff to work effectively in cross-cultural situations; it means being respectful and responsive to the health beliefs, practices, and cultural/linguistic needs of diverse populations. Cultural humility (Bates) is a process requiring humility as individuals continually engage in self-reflection and self-critique as lifelong learners and reflective practitioners - 'the more you know about a particular culture, the more you realize you don't know.'
Q. Know what cultural humility requires and the problem with competence.Cultural humility requires respect, acknowledging both differences and similarities, dynamic communication, and honest self-assessment; it commits to self-evaluation, fixing power imbalances, and developing partnerships/advocating for others. The problem with 'competence' is that it suggests culture can be reduced to a technical skill and results in lists of 'do's and don'ts' for treating patients of a given ethnic background.
Q. Know the cross-cultural approach and the Golden Rule.A 'Cross Cultural Approach' uses communication skills, awareness of cross-cutting cultural and social issues, and recognizing health beliefs as universal human beliefs, needs, and traits. The Golden Rule: treat patients as we would want to be treated during periods of vulnerability and fear; patient centeredness is built on respect, sensitivity, composure, partnership, honesty, curiosity, and tolerance.
Q. How does the deck define intersectionality?How race, class, gender, and other characteristics intersect and overlap to create unique experiences — Intersectionality: overlapping identities create unique sets of experiences.
Q. Per Bates as cited in the deck, cultural humility is best described as:A process requiring ongoing self-reflection and self-critique as lifelong learners — Bates: humility is a process of continual self-reflection and self-critique.
Q. What does the deck identify as 'the problem with cultural competence'?It suggests culture can be reduced to a technical skill and produces lists of 'do's and don'ts' — Competence implies culture is a technical skill and yields do/don't lists.
Q. Which statement reflects the spirit of cultural humility as the deck frames it?'The more you know about a particular culture, the more you realize you don't know' — Humility slide: the more you know, the more you realize you don't know.
Q. The 'Golden Rule' the Cultural deck closes with states that patient-centeredness is built on:Respect, sensitivity, composure, partnership, honesty, curiosity, and tolerance — Patient centeredness = respect, sensitivity, composure, partnership, honesty, curiosity, tolerance.

SOAP & Documentation 7

Q. Know the 4 sections of a SOAP note and what each contains.SOAP has 4 sections: Subjective, Objective, Assessment, Plan. Subjective = the History section (HPI); contains the chief complaint and history of present illness, the patient's perception of the problem, and how the problem has evolved. It also includes past medical history, social history, and sexual history. Objective = values, Physical Exam (PE), and data gathered (vital signs; status/appearance on presentation; physical exam; laboratory data). Assessment = diagnoses and differential diagnoses (Dx's). Plan = the action plan.
Q. Know what a narrative is, the use of abbreviations, and the other subjective components.A narrative is the patient's history written in paragraph form (not bullet points), logically composed and coherent. Medical abbreviations may be used in SOAP notes or narratives for efficiency. Bates' basic HPI template: opening statement (CC in clinical context), elaborate description of the CC, accompanying symptomatology, absent pertinent symptomatology, pertinent PMH/FH/social history, and a concluding statement of how the patient got to the care site. Other subjective components beyond the HPI include allergies, PMH (chronic conditions, trauma, hospitalizations), FHx, SHx (surgical), SoHx (social), sexual history, immunizations, and review of systems.
Q. In a SOAP note, physical exam findings, vital signs, and laboratory data belong to which section?Objective — Objective = values, Physical Exam (PE), and data gathered.
Q. Which SOAP section contains the diagnoses and differential diagnoses?Assessment — Assessment = Diagnoses, Differential Dx's.
Q. In the SOAP note, the 'action plan' is documented under which section?Plan — Plan = Action plan.
Q. A patient's own description of how a problem has evolved is best classified as which type of data?Subjective — Subjective contains the patient's perception of the problem and description of how the problem has evolved.
Q. Per the slides, pain that is 'severely restricting ADLs' would be classified as:Severe — Severe = severely restricting ADLs (Mild = doesn't interfere; Moderate = starting to interfere).

History Components 27

Q. Know the components of a history and what makes a reliable historian.Under Subjective, the full history includes: Chief Complaint, HPI, Past Medical History, Family History, Social/Lifestyle/Sexual, and Review of Systems. Documentation should 'paint a picture' - any clinician reading the HPI should have a relatively clear picture of why the patient presented. The person providing the history is the 'historian.' Consider reliability: a patient historian should be alert, competent, and have intact memory (impaired by medications, substance use, stress, Alzheimer's, or dementia); a non-patient historian (parent, caretaker, translator) should be informed, trustworthy, and reliable.
Q. Know how to elicit and document the Chief Complaint.The CC is the patient's response to questions like 'So..., what brings you in today?' Even if the CC is on the intake form, confirm the problem and ask the patient to reiterate it for accuracy. When possible, directly quote the patient's own words (e.g., 'it feels like there is a brick resting on my right upper back and shoulder area'). If a caregiver provides the CC (children or patients unable to respond), document the source of the information (e.g., 'Caregiver (Niece, Doris Jones) reports...').
Q. Know the definition of the HPI and every letter of LOPQRST.The HPI is 'a complete, clear, chronologic description of the problem(s) prompting the patient's visit.' LOPQRST = 'The How.' L: Location; O: Onset of complaint; P: Prior, Palliative, Provocative, Progression; Q: Quality of pain; R: Radiation; S: Severity; T: Timing. The expanded summary is LOPPPPQRST (priors, palliative, provocative, progression). Location: ask where and have the patient point to the area (patients may use incorrect anatomical vocabulary). Onset: when it started (day/week/month/year, time) and mode of injury. Progression: since it started, improved, worsened, or stayed the same. Prior: first time or not; if not, first occurrence, frequency, duration, prior treatments, other doctors seen. Palliative: what has helped and what was most helpful. Provocative: what makes it worse (position, movement, medications, foods, modalities, surgeries, time). Quality: describe the symptom - pain (sharp/dull/achy/burning; throbbing/pounding/pulsating), paresthesias (numbness/tingling/pins & needles), weakness, fatigue, dizziness. Radiation = the symptom travels from its point of origin; Referral = the symptom is located in a different place than its actual cause. Severity: 0-10 Numeric/Visual Analogue Scale (at onset, current, best, worst). Mild = doesn't interfere with ADLs; Moderate = starting to interfere; Severe = severely restricting ADLs. Timing/Temporal: constant or intermittent; if intermittent, note frequency and duration of episodes.
Q. Know the Past Medical History components and every letter of FAOMASH.PMH components on the slides: chronic conditions, allergies, hospitalizations, trauma, medications, FHx (family history), SHx (surgical history), SoHx (social history), sexual history, and review of systems. The slides DO spell out FAOMASH: F = Family history, A = Allergies, O = Other illness (chronic conditions) and/or other doctors/specialists seen (not necessarily related to the CC), M = Medications, A = Accidents, S = Surgeries, H = Hospitalizations.
Q. Know how to take family history, allergies, chronic illness, and medications.Family history focuses on immediate family (age and health status of parents, siblings, children; grandparents if known); if deceased, ask the cause of death. Major focus: does anyone have diabetes, heart disease, strokes, cancer, arthritis, or other conditions. Allergy status should be confirmed for each patient at each visit, and for certain before prescribing any medication; categories include medications, latex, environment, and foods; if allergic, ask the type of reaction (hives, swelling, airway obstruction). Other chronic illnesses to ask about: hypertension, diabetes, heart disease, COPD, peripheral artery disease, thyroid disorders, GI disorders, mental health conditions, orthopedic conditions, cancer. For medications ask: what is taken, why, dosage (amount and frequency), and how long; list immunizations with date and reaction (or document 'per patient recall').
Q. Know how to ask about accidents, surgeries, and hospitalizations.Accidents: when they occurred, how they happened, the injuries, whether medical care was given, and whether injuries healed or are ongoing. Surgeries: which procedure, when, the reason, results, and complications. Hospitalizations: when, why (reason/discharge diagnosis), duration, treatment received (procedures/tests/specialty consults), and residual problems. Hospitalizations and surgeries are NOT synonymous - you cannot ask one to cover both.

ROS 13

Q. Know the purpose of the Review of Systems.The Review of Systems is a discussion of the various body organ systems that may uncover problems not already mentioned, or pertinent negatives that support your diagnosis or help streamline your differential diagnoses. Patients often leave out pertinent facts of their history because they do not realize the connection to a problem; the ROS reveals the presence or absence of common symptoms of major body systems. The ROS reviews the patient history in another format by asking 'head to toe' related questions.
Q. Know the correct format and timing of the Review of Systems.It is acceptable to use closed-ended 'yes or no' questions in the ROS. Questions vary based on the patient's age, complaints, and general state of health, using your clinical judgement. ROS questions can be asked during the physical exam of that body area (e.g., GI questions during the abdominal exam, musculoskeletal questions during the spine exam). You may uncover major health events during the ROS that should be added to the History of Present Illness or Past Medical History.
Q. Know the full list of ROS body systems.Systems addressed: Constitutional/General, Skin, HEENT (Head, Eyes, Ears, Nose/Sinuses, Mouth/Throat, Neck), Cardiovascular, Respiratory, Gastrointestinal, Genitourinary, Musculoskeletal, Neurologic, Psychiatric, Endocrine, and Hematologic/Lymphatic. Stay organized and move through your list in an organized fashion; generally, working top down is helpful.
Q. Know the skin ABCDE screen and what else the skin ROS covers.For skin lesions/moles, use ABCDE: Asymmetry, Borders, Color, Diameter, Evolving (is the lesion changing?). Skin screening also covers rashes, lumps, sores, itching, dryness, color change (cyanosis, rubor, jaundice), and changes in hair or nails (cracking, spooning, clubbing).
Q. Know the Assessment step and Bates' process for identifying problems.After the history and physical, assessment is where you analyze the data and move from description and observation to analysis and interpretation. Bates' steps for identifying problems: identify abnormal findings, localize them anatomically, cluster the clinical findings, search for the probable cause, generate hypotheses, then test them to establish a working diagnosis.
Q. What is the primary purpose of performing a Review of Systems (ROS)?To uncover problems not already mentioned and reveal the presence or absence of common symptoms of major body systems — The ROS reveals presence/absence of common symptoms and uncovers problems or pertinent negatives.
Q. Which question format does the ROS deck say is acceptable to use during the Review of Systems?Closed-ended 'yes or no' questions — The slide states it is acceptable to use closed-ended yes/no questions.
Q. According to the deck, when may ROS questions be appropriately asked?During the physical exam of that body area, e.g., GI questions during the abdominal exam — ROS questions can be asked during the physical exam of that body area.
Q. The 'ABCDE' mnemonic taught in the Skin portion of the ROS refers to what?Asymmetry, Borders, Color, Diameter, Evolving — For moles/lesions: Asymmetry, Borders, Color, Diameter, Evolving.
Q. A patient reports needing extra pillows to sleep and shortness of breath when lying flat. In which ROS system are these screening findings addressed?Cardiovascular — Orthopnea (number of pillows) and PND are cardiovascular ROS items.
Q. Which set of symptoms belongs to the Constitutional/General portion of the ROS?Unintentional weight change, weakness, fatigue, fever, and night sweats — General ROS covers weight change, weakness, fatigue, fever, night sweats, sleep changes.
Q. Intermittent claudication and varicose veins are screening items under which ROS heading?Peripheral Vascular — The Peripheral Vascular slide asks about claudication, varicose veins, clots, and swelling.
Q. According to the Assessment portion, what are the first steps in Bates' process for identifying problems?Identify abnormal findings and localize them anatomically — Bates' steps begin with identifying abnormal findings and localizing anatomically.

Sexual & Social 16

Q. Know why a sexual history is taken for all patients.Sexual health history is an important part of a routine medical exam for ALL patients, regardless of gender identity or sexual orientation. Sexual health is integral to general health; it is associated with happiness, well-being, and longevity, and may indicate psychiatric or other medical disorders or the need for primary prevention (immunizations, contraception, PEP, PrEP).
Q. Know the difference between sex and gender, and orientation vs gender identity.Sex refers to biological differences (anatomy, chromosomes, hormones, genes); gender refers to social and cultural distinctions (gender identity, expression, roles). Sexual orientation and gender identity are NOT the same; all people have both, and how people identify can change.
Q. Know how to establish the relationship before a sexual history.Establish rapport with effective listening and mirroring (names, pronouns, body parts), an individualized/holistic approach, and by asking permission: ask if it is ok to discuss a sexual history and be transparent about why you are asking. Start all appointments with clothes ON, and approach trauma experiences slowly and at a time when support can be offered.
Q. Know how to use non-judgmental, patient-centered language.Take an anatomic inventory and establish which words you and the patient will use: 'When referring to your genitals, are there specific terms that you use?' Use open-ended questions to capture diverse sexual behaviors, validate all practices by asking about both high and low risk activities (and pleasures), and avoid assumptions about what kinds of sex someone has.
Q. Know the CDC 5 P's and the Fenway 8 P's.The CDC's 5 P's of the sexual history are: Partners, Practices, Protection for STDs, Past history of STDs, and Prevention of pregnancy. Fenway expands these to 8 P's: Preferences, Partners, Practices, Protection for STIs, Past history of STIs, Pregnancy, Pleasure, and Partner Violence.
Q. Know the trauma-informed care approach and how to respond to disclosure.Any exam or procedure has the potential to be traumatizing; before the exam, review prior experiences, explain the purpose, offer to talk through the steps, and remind the patient 'You're in control here. If you want me to stop at any point, please let me know.' Responding to disclosure: Listen and communicate belief ('That must have been frightening'), validate the decision to disclose, emphasize that violence is unacceptable, be clear the patient is not to blame ('What happened is not your fault'), and make a safety plan / provide resources.
Q. Know why you ask everyone and how to reduce stigma.Ask ALL people and make it policy - asking everyone normalizes the questions; nearly 80% of providers believed patients would refuse to disclose orientation, but only about 10% of patients said they would refuse. Everyone has activities they find 'icky'; we cannot eliminate our bias by pretending not to have any - the guidance is to 'find your Yucks and fix your face.'
Q. According to the Fenway presentation, sexual health history should be taken from which patients?ALL patients, regardless of gender identity or sexual orientation — Sexual health history is important for ALL patients regardless of gender identity/orientation.
Q. How does the Fenway deck distinguish sex from gender?Sex refers to biological differences; gender refers to social and cultural distinctions — Sex = biological differences; gender = social/cultural distinctions.
Q. The CDC's '5 P's' of the sexual history, per the Fenway deck, are:Partners, Practices, Protection for STDs, Past history of STDs, and Prevention of pregnancy — CDC 5 P's: Partners, Practices, Protection for STDs, Past history of STDs, Prevention of pregnancy.
Q. Fenway expands the CDC's 5 P's into '8 P's' by adding which additional P's?Preferences, Pleasure, and Partner Violence — The 8 P's add Preferences, Pleasure, and Partner Violence.
Q. Before beginning a sexual history, the Fenway deck recommends the clinician:Ask if it is ok to discuss a sexual history and be transparent about why you are asking — Ask permission and explain why; start appointments with clothes on.
Q. Which is an example of taking an 'anatomic inventory' with patient-centered language?'When referring to your genitals, are there specific terms that you use?' — Establish shared terms: ask what words the patient uses for their genitals.
Q. Why does the Fenway deck recommend asking sexual orientation/gender questions of EVERYONE?Asking everyone normalizes the questions and reduces stigma and assumptions — Ask all people and make it policy - it normalizes questions and decreases stigma.
Q. In a trauma-informed exam, which phrase does the Fenway deck recommend?'You're in control here. If you want me to stop at any point, please let me know.' — Trauma-informed: emphasize patient control and the ability to stop.
Q. When a patient discloses a history of violence, the Fenway trauma-informed approach advises the clinician to:Communicate belief, emphasize that violence is unacceptable, and be clear the patient is not to blame — Responding to disclosure: believe, validate, state violence is unacceptable, not their fault.
